# Scoring Rubric — 0 to 100

Score each pillar independently, then sum. Within each pillar, score each
line item, then add them up. When a company sits between two bands, use
judgment and round to a whole number. When data is missing, score that line
at the bottom of the middle band (not zero, not benefit of the doubt).

Sector context matters: compare margins and returns against the company's
industry, not the whole market. A 10% net margin is weak for software and
excellent for a grocer. When the rubric says "vs. peers," that means the
2–4 closest competitors.

---

## Pillar 1 — Profitability & business quality (25 points)

The single most important pillar. Great long-term holdings are almost
always businesses that earn high returns on the money they employ, year
after year.

**ROIC (or ROE if ROIC unavailable) — 10 pts**
- 9–10: ROIC consistently >20%
- 7–8: 15–20%
- 5–6: 10–15%
- 3–4: 7–10%, or good level but erratic
- 0–2: <7% (business earns less than its cost of capital — value destroyer)

**Margins vs. peers and vs. own history — 8 pts**
- 7–8: Operating margin above peer average AND stable or expanding
- 5–6: Around peer average, stable
- 3–4: Below peers, or visibly compressing
- 0–2: Thin/negative margins with no structural reason to improve

**Free cash flow conversion — 7 pts**
FCF / net income over the last 3 years:
- 6–7: FCF ≥ 90% of net income (earnings are real cash)
- 4–5: 70–90%
- 2–3: 40–70% (capex-heavy or working-capital hungry)
- 0–1: Persistently negative FCF or FCF far below reported earnings
  (earnings quality concern)

## Pillar 2 — Growth (20 points)

Growth matters, but *consistent* growth matters more than spectacular
growth. One blowout year is noise; eight years of 10% is a machine.

**Revenue growth, 5-yr CAGR — 8 pts**
- 7–8: >12% and reasonably steady
- 5–6: 6–12%
- 3–4: 2–6% (roughly GDP-ish)
- 0–2: Flat or shrinking

**EPS / FCF-per-share growth, 5-yr — 8 pts**
Per-share matters: buybacks help, dilution hurts.
- 7–8: >15% CAGR
- 5–6: 8–15%
- 3–4: 2–8%
- 0–2: Flat, shrinking, or negative earnings

**Consistency & visibility — 4 pts**
- 4: Grew revenue in ≥4 of last 5 years; recurring or contracted revenue
- 2–3: Mostly steady with one bad year (e.g., 2020-style shock)
- 0–1: Boom-bust, cyclical whipsaws, or heavy customer concentration

## Pillar 3 — Financial health (15 points)

The balance sheet decides who survives recessions. Debt is what turns a
bad year into a catastrophe.

**Leverage — 8 pts**
Net debt / EBITDA (or debt/equity if EBITDA negative):
- 7–8: Net cash, or net debt/EBITDA < 1x
- 5–6: 1–2x
- 3–4: 2–3x
- 0–2: >3x, or rising leverage funding dividends/buybacks

**Interest coverage — 4 pts**
Operating income / interest expense:
- 4: >10x or essentially no debt
- 2–3: 4–10x
- 0–1: <4x

**Liquidity — 3 pts**
- 3: Current ratio >1.5, ample cash
- 2: 1.0–1.5
- 0–1: <1.0 or reliant on rolling short-term debt

## Pillar 4 — Competitive moat (15 points)

Qualitative but not hand-wavy: a real moat leaves fingerprints in the
numbers (stable high ROIC, stable/growing market share, pricing power
shown by margins holding through inflation).

**Moat source & strength — 10 pts**
Identify which of these actually applies (be skeptical — most companies
have none): network effects, switching costs, brand pricing power, cost
advantages/scale, regulatory licenses, patents/IP.
- 9–10: Dominant position + at least two durable moat sources, evidenced
  in the numbers for 5+ years
- 6–8: Clear leader with one strong moat source
- 3–5: Decent position but replicable; competes mainly on execution
- 0–2: Commodity business, price-taker, or share losses to rivals

**Threat picture — 5 pts**
- 4–5: No credible threat to the core business in the next 5 years
- 2–3: Real but manageable threats (disruption risk, strong #2 player)
- 0–1: Core business actively being disrupted or regulated away

## Pillar 5 — Valuation (15 points)

Quality first, but price still matters — overpaying for a great business
turns a good idea into a mediocre return. Judge valuation against the
company's own history and its growth rate, not a universal "P/E under 15"
rule.

**Cyclicals: normalize first.** For commodity producers (miners, oil &
gas, shippers) and deep cyclicals (ag equipment, semis, homebuilders), a
low P/E at peak earnings is a trap and a high P/E at trough earnings can
be a bargain. Estimate mid-cycle earnings (e.g., average the last full
cycle, or reprice at long-run average commodity prices) and score the
multiple on THAT, not the optical trailing P/E. Say explicitly in the
report which basis was used and why.

**Multiple vs. own 5-yr history and peers — 8 pts**
Use P/E and P/FCF (EV/EBITDA for capital-intensive firms):
- 7–8: Trading clearly below its own historical average and/or peers
  without a business-quality reason
- 5–6: Roughly in line with history and peers
- 3–4: 20–50% above historical norms
- 0–2: Extreme multiple (e.g., >50x earnings without hypergrowth), or
  unprofitable with valuation resting entirely on a story

**Growth-adjusted check — 7 pts**
PEG-style sanity check (P/E ÷ expected EPS growth) plus FCF yield:
- 6–7: PEG < 1.5 or FCF yield > 5%
- 4–5: PEG 1.5–2.5 or FCF yield 3–5%
- 2–3: PEG 2.5–4 or FCF yield 1–3%
- 0–1: PEG > 4, negative FCF, or negative earnings

## Pillar 6 — Management & shareholder friendliness (10 points)

**Capital allocation track record — 5 pts**
- 4–5: Buybacks at sensible prices and/or well-integrated acquisitions;
  dividend (if any) growing and comfortably covered (payout <60% of FCF)
- 2–3: Mixed record; some overpriced deals or pauses in returns
- 0–1: Serial diluter, empire-building M&A, dividend funded by debt

**Share count & alignment — 5 pts**
- 4–5: Share count flat or shrinking over 5 years; meaningful insider
  ownership; no accounting controversies
- 2–3: Mild dilution (<2%/yr, typical stock comp)
- 0–1: Heavy dilution (>4%/yr), insider selling waves, restatements,
  auditor changes, or SEC inquiries

## Red-flag overrides (apply after summing)

These cap the final score no matter how good the rest looks. Use the
lowest applicable cap and state the override explicitly in the report.

| Red flag | Score cap |
|---|---|
| Going-concern doubt, or likely need to raise cash within 12 months just to operate | 25 |
| Accounting restatement, fraud allegations from credible short reports, or auditor resignation in past 2 years | 30 |
| Persistently negative FCF **and** net debt/EBITDA > 3x | 35 |
| Single customer or product >40% of revenue with contract/patent expiry risk | 55 |
| Core product in structural decline (not cyclical — think film cameras, not housing) | 55 |
| Dividend payout >100% of FCF for 2+ years | 60 |

## Calibration notes

- A "steady blue chip fairly priced" (think classic consumer staple)
  should land around 65–75, not 90. Reserve 85+ for truly exceptional
  businesses at sensible prices — most years there are only a handful.
- Don't anchor the score to the stock's popularity or recent performance.
  The rubric scores the business and the price, not the narrative.
- Cyclicals at peak earnings rarely deserve more than 7/15 on valuation
  even when the trailing P/E looks cheap — the E is inflated, not the
  price attractive. The reverse applies at genuine troughs.
- If two pillars contradict (great business, scary valuation), let both
  show — that's exactly the tension the total is designed to express.
