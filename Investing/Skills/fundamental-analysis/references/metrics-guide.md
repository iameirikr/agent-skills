# Plain-English Metrics Guide

Ready-made one-line explanations for the report. Reuse these (lightly
adapted to the company) so explanations stay consistent across reports.
Format in reports: **Metric: value** — explanation. ✅/⚠️/❌

## Profitability

- **Revenue** — all the money that came in the door before any costs.
- **Gross margin** — what's left after the direct cost of making the
  product. High = the product itself is very profitable. (Software 70%+,
  retail often under 30% — compare within the industry.)
- **Operating margin** — profit after running the whole business (staff,
  offices, marketing). The best single "is this business efficient" number.
- **Net margin** — the cut of every $1 of sales that ends up as profit.
- **EPS (earnings per share)** — total profit divided by shares; your
  slice of the earnings for each share you own.
- **ROIC (return on invested capital)** — the interest rate the business
  earns on money it reinvests in itself. Over 15% for years = a
  quality machine; below ~7% it's destroying value by growing.
- **ROE (return on equity)** — profit as a % of shareholders' money in
  the business. Like ROIC but can be inflated by debt, so check both.
- **Free cash flow (FCF)** — actual cash left after paying all bills and
  investments; the money that can fund dividends, buybacks, or growth.
  Earnings are an opinion, cash is a fact.
- **FCF conversion** — how much of reported profit becomes real cash.
  Near 100% = honest earnings; far below = profits exist on paper only.

## Growth

- **Revenue CAGR** — the smoothed average yearly growth rate over the
  period ("grew ~9% a year for 5 years").
- **Per-share growth** — growth after accounting for shares created or
  bought back. What *your* slice grew, which is what actually matters.
- **Recurring revenue** — subscription/contract income that repeats
  without re-selling. Makes future revenue far more predictable.

## Financial health

- **Net debt** — total debt minus cash. Negative ("net cash") means they
  could pay off every loan tomorrow and have money left.
- **Net debt / EBITDA** — roughly how many years of cash earnings it
  would take to clear the debt. Under 1x is a fortress; over 3x means a
  bad stretch could get dangerous.
- **Interest coverage** — how many times over yearly profit covers the
  interest bill. Over 10x = comfortable; under 4x = the bank owns their
  bad years.
- **Current ratio** — near-term assets vs. near-term bills. Above 1.5 =
  comfortable cushion; below 1.0 = counting on money arriving on time.

## Moat

- **Moat** — a durable reason competitors can't steal the profits:
  switching costs (painful to leave), network effects (more users = more
  value), brand pricing power (can raise prices without losing customers),
  scale/cost advantage (can profitably sell cheaper than rivals),
  licenses/patents (legally protected).
- The test: if a competitor with $10B tried to take this market, what
  would actually stop them?

## Valuation

- **P/E (price-to-earnings)** — the price of $1 of annual profit. A P/E
  of 25 = paying $25 per $1 of yearly earnings. Only meaningful vs. the
  company's growth, its own history, and its peers.
- **Forward P/E** — same, but on next year's expected earnings.
- **P/FCF** — price of $1 of actual annual cash flow; harder to
  manipulate than P/E.
- **EV/EBITDA** — a debt-aware price tag; useful for comparing companies
  with different debt loads.
- **PEG** — P/E divided by growth rate. Roughly: under ~1.5 you're paying
  a fair price for the growth; over ~3 you're paying for a best-case
  future.
- **FCF yield** — cash flow as a % of price; like an interest rate the
  stock "pays" you. 5%+ is cheap for a quality business.
- **Dividend yield** — cash paid to you per year as a % of the price.
- **Payout ratio** — the slice of profit (or better, FCF) paid as
  dividends. Under 60% = safe and growable; over 100% = living beyond
  its means.

## Ownership & management

- **Share count trend** — shrinking = buybacks increasing your slice;
  growing = dilution shrinking it. Silent but powerful over a decade.
- **Insider ownership** — executives owning a real stake means they win
  and lose with you.
- **Capital allocation** — what management does with the cash: reinvest,
  acquire, buy back, or pay dividends. The skill that separates great
  CEOs from average ones.

## Signals

- ✅ healthy for this type of business
- ⚠️ worth watching / middling
- ❌ a real problem
